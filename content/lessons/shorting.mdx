## Going in reverse

Most traders buy first, sell later. Short sellers do it backwards: **sell first, buy later**. The goal is to sell high and buy back low — pocketing the difference.

## How it works mechanically

1. You **borrow** shares from your broker (they lend you shares other customers own)
2. You **sell** those shares immediately at the current price
3. Later, you **buy** the same number of shares back (called "covering")
4. You **return** the shares to the broker
5. Your profit = what you sold for minus what you bought back for

**Example:** TSLA is at $300. You think it's overpriced.

- You short 10 shares → you receive $3,000
- TSLA drops to $240
- You buy 10 shares back for $2,400
- You return the shares
- **Profit: $600**

## The asymmetric risk

Going long (buying a stock), the worst that happens is it goes to $0. You lose what you put in — that's it.

Short selling has **no ceiling on losses**. A stock can theoretically go up forever.

**Example:** You short AAPL at $200 with 20 shares ($4,000 received). AAPL rockets to $350. To cover, you need to pay $7,000 — a **$3,000 loss** on a $4,000 position. That's a 75% loss, and the stock could keep climbing.

This is why short squeezes are so violent. When a heavily shorted stock rises, short sellers are forced to buy back shares to limit losses — which drives the price up even more, forcing more shorts to cover. It's a feedback loop.

## Short selling in this app

This app runs on Alpaca paper trading which supports short positions. When you sell a stock you don't own, you're shorting it. Your position detail will show **SHORT** and your P&L moves opposite to price — when the stock falls, you profit.

## Who uses short selling

- **Hedge funds** hedging long positions against market downturns
- **Traders** betting against overvalued companies
- **Arbitrageurs** exploiting pricing inefficiencies

Retail traders who short individual stocks are usually speculating — it's high risk, high reward.

## Key takeaway

**Short selling lets you profit from falling prices, but the risk is theoretically unlimited.** Use it carefully, with position sizes you can afford to be wrong about.
