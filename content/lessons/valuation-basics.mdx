## Price ≠ value

A $5 stock isn't cheap. A $500 stock isn't expensive. Price per share is meaningless without context. What matters is what you're getting for that price.

## The P/E ratio

**P/E (Price-to-Earnings)** is the most common valuation metric. It tells you how much investors are paying for each dollar of earnings.

**Formula:** Stock price ÷ Earnings per share (EPS)

**Example:** AAPL trades at $200. Their earnings per share is $6.50.

> P/E = $200 ÷ $6.50 = **~30.8**

That means investors are paying $30.80 for every $1 of annual profit. Whether that's reasonable depends on how fast earnings are growing.

## What P/E tells you

- **Low P/E (under ~15):** Market expects slow or no growth — or thinks the company is in trouble
- **Average P/E (~20–25):** Roughly in line with S&P 500 historical average
- **High P/E (30+):** Market expects strong future growth — you're paying for what comes next, not just today's earnings

A high P/E isn't automatically bad. Fast-growing companies deserve higher multiples. The question is: will they grow into that valuation?

**Example:** A tech startup with P/E of 80 might be fair if revenue is growing 50% per year. A slow utility company with P/E of 80 is almost certainly overpriced.

## Market cap

**Market cap = Share price × Total shares outstanding**

This is the total value of a company.

| Tier | Market Cap | Examples |
|---|---|---|
| Mega-cap | $1T+ | AAPL, MSFT, NVDA |
| Large-cap | $10B–$1T | Most S&P 500 companies |
| Mid-cap | $2B–$10B | Solid established companies |
| Small-cap | Under $2B | Higher risk, higher potential |

Bigger companies are generally more stable but grow slower. Small-caps can move 20% in a day — up or down.

## P/E has limits

Some companies have no earnings at all (startups, loss-making growers). P/E is undefined for them. Analysts use other metrics: **P/S (price-to-sales)**, **EV/EBITDA**, or simply growth rate comparisons.

P/E also doesn't account for debt. A company with $10B in debt and a low P/E might be a value trap, not a bargain.

## Key takeaway

**Don't look at share price to judge value — look at P/E and market cap.** A "cheap" stock with a P/E of 5 might be cheap for a reason. An "expensive" stock with a P/E of 40 might be exactly right for a fast-growing business.
