## Two kinds of profit

Your portfolio shows two P&L numbers: **unrealized** and **realized**. They look similar but they're fundamentally different.

## Unrealized P&L

Unrealized P&L is what you'd make *if you sold right now*. It's a paper number — the market's current opinion of your position.

**Formula:** (Current price − Avg entry price) × Shares held

**Example:** You bought 10 shares of AAPL at $190. It's now trading at $215.

> Unrealized P&L = ($215 − $190) × 10 = **+$250**

That $250 isn't in your pocket yet. If AAPL drops back to $190 tomorrow, it's gone.

## Realized P&L

Realized P&L is money you've actually locked in by selling. Once you close a position, the gain or loss is permanent.

**Example:** You sell those 10 AAPL shares at $215.

> Realized P&L = ($215 − $190) × 10 = **+$250 — now in your cash balance**

No matter what AAPL does next, you've captured that $250.

## The psychology trap

Watching unrealized gains is where most beginners go wrong. A stock up 40% feels like a win — but until you sell, the market can take it back. Many traders hold too long waiting for "just a little more" and end up selling for less (or at a loss) than they could have.

The flip side: selling too early to lock in small gains while big winners run away from you.

**There's no rule for when to sell — but understanding the difference between paper gains and real gains is the first step.**

## Averaging down

If you buy more shares after a stock drops, your average entry price falls. This can lower your unrealized loss — but it also means more money at risk.

**Example:** Bought 5 AAPL at $200, then 5 more at $180 → avg entry = $190. Current price $175 → loss is now on 10 shares, not 5.

## Key takeaway

**Unrealized P&L is the score. Realized P&L is the result.** Don't celebrate (or panic) until you've actually closed the position.
