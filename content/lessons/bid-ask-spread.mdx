## Two prices, always

Every stock has two prices at any moment:

- **Bid** — the highest price a buyer is willing to pay
- **Ask** (or offer) — the lowest price a seller is willing to accept

When you buy, you pay the ask. When you sell, you receive the bid. The gap between them is the **spread**.

**Example:** AAPL bid $199.95 / ask $200.05 → spread = $0.10

## Why the spread costs you money

Say you buy 100 shares of AAPL at the ask ($200.05) then immediately try to sell them. You'd sell at the bid ($199.95). You'd lose $10 — even though the stock didn't move at all.

The spread is a round-trip cost embedded in every trade. It doesn't show up as a line-item fee, which is why people miss it.

**On 100 shares with a $0.10 spread:** $10 cost to open + $10 cost to close = **$20 invisible fee per round trip**

## Who captures the spread?

Market makers — firms that post both a bid and ask simultaneously. They profit on the difference by constantly buying at the bid and selling at the ask. In exchange, they provide liquidity: there's always someone to trade with.

## Liquid vs illiquid stocks

For liquid stocks like AAPL or MSFT, spreads are tiny — often $0.01. Millions of shares trade every day so competition keeps spreads tight.

For thinly traded small-cap stocks, spreads can be $0.50, $1, or more. On a $10 stock, a $0.50 spread is **5% of your money gone before the stock moves at all.**

## How to minimize spread costs

- **Stick to liquid stocks** with high daily volume — this app shows volume on every stock page
- **Use limit orders** to buy at the bid and sell at the ask instead of crossing the spread
- **Avoid pre/after-hours trading** where spreads widen dramatically because fewer participants are trading

## Key takeaway

**Every trade costs you the spread, even if you have zero commission.** On liquid stocks it's negligible. On thinly traded stocks it can kill your returns before you even start.
