## What you're about to do

You're going to buy a stock. Not pretend — you're going to pick a real company, decide on an amount, and place an actual order in the market. The money is paper (it's not real cash), but every other part of this is exactly how it works.

## Picking a stock

Don't overthink this. Think of companies whose products you use every day:

- **AAPL** — Apple. If you have an iPhone, you use this company's product daily.
- **MSFT** — Microsoft. Windows, Xbox, Teams.
- **NVDA** — Nvidia. The chips inside AI, gaming, data centers.
- **NKE** — Nike. Shoes, gear.
- **SBUX** — Starbucks. Coffee.

There's no wrong answer here. The goal is to get your first order placed and understand what it feels like.

## Shares vs Dollars

On the trade page, you'll see two modes:

**Shares** — you decide how many shares to buy. If AAPL is $200 and you buy 2 shares, that's $400 invested.

**Dollars** — you invest a fixed dollar amount and get however many shares (or fractions) that buys. $100 in AAPL at $200/share = 0.5 shares. This is called a fractional or notional order.

Use **Dollars** if you want to invest a specific amount. Use **Shares** if you want a specific number of shares.

## What a market order does

When you tap **Buy** with Market selected, your order goes straight to the exchange and fills immediately at the current best available price. You'll see it in your positions within seconds.

The price you pay might be a cent or two different from what you saw — that's normal. It's called slippage, and on liquid stocks like AAPL it's tiny.

## Your turn

Head to the trade page, search your stock, and place the order. This lesson completes automatically once your buy order goes through — no need to come back here.

> This lesson requires the market to be open (9:30am–4:00pm ET, Monday–Friday) to mark complete.
